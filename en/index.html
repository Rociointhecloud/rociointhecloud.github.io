How I work when reality isn’t tidy

In my most recent training in Data Analytics, I realised something important: learning data isn’t about mastering tools, it’s about building judgement when time is short, context is missing, and you still have to respond.

1) Questions that save weeks of work

Before opening a notebook: what decision does this support, who will use it, and what does each KPI actually mean? I don’t mind seeming persistent if it prevents flawed conclusions.

2) Cleaning with intent

For me, there’s no such thing as “correct” cleaning. There is cleaning that respects the business. That’s why I document the reasoning behind every change and leave repeatable steps (Power Query / pipelines).

3) Exploration to spot traps

Distributions, outliers, relationships, bias, temporal consistency… If something “looks fine” but doesn’t make sense, I stop and review it.

4) Solutions that can be maintained

I value what’s explainable and stable. I’d rather build something useful today and improvable tomorrow than something spectacular that no one can sustain.

5) Reporting that guides

I come from a marketing background, so yes: aesthetics matter to me. But not as decoration — as clarity. If the visual hierarchy doesn’t guide the reader or the experience doesn’t support the decision, I know something hasn’t been thought through properly. Aesthetics aren’t an add-on: they’re part of the decision-making system.

6) Teamwork without surprises

Git/GitHub, documentation, and serious care with credentials and sensitive data. I take responsibility for not leaving problems behind for the next person.
